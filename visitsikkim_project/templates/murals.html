Sacred Murals of Sikkim

Digitized collections of breathtaking wall paintings and thangkas preserved within the monastery walls.

Artworks in This Archive

The Life of Guru Rinpoche

Located at Rumtek Monastery

This vast and intricate mural is a visual biography of **Guru Rinpoche**, also known as Padmasambhava, the "Lotus-Born" master who is revered as the second Buddha. Located in the main assembly hall, it serves as a source of inspiration and a teaching tool for monks and pilgrims alike. The artwork is executed in the traditional Tibetan style, characterized by its vibrant colors, precise line work, and sacred geometry.

The paints are made from ground mineral pigments—lapis lazuli for blue, malachite for green, and cinnabar for red—mixed with animal glue, a technique that has allowed these artworks to endure for centuries. The mural is read from one section to the next, narrating key events such as the Guru's miraculous birth from a lotus flower, his subjugation of local deities who opposed Buddhism, and his establishment of the first monastery at Samye in Tibet. Studying this mural is an act of devotion, connecting the viewer to the very source of their faith.

The Wheel of Life (Bhavacakra)

Located at Pemayangtse Monastery

Often painted near the entrance of a monastery, the **Bhavacakra**, or Wheel of Life, is one of the most profound teaching murals in Buddhism. It is a symbolic representation of **samsara**—the endless, suffering-filled cycle of birth, death, and rebirth to which all beings are bound. The entire wheel is held in the clutches of **Yama**, the fearsome lord of death, symbolizing the impermanence and suffering inherent in worldly existence.

The mural is divided into sections. At the hub are three animals—a pig, a snake, and a rooster—representing the three root poisons of ignorance, aversion, and attachment. The next layer depicts the six realms of existence: the realms of gods, demigods, humans, animals, hungry ghosts, and hell beings. The outer rim illustrates the twelve links of dependent origination, the chain of causation that traps beings in samsara. The mural serves as a powerful daily reminder to practitioners to strive for liberation from this cycle.

Mandala of Compassion

Located at Tashiding Monastery

A **mandala** is a sacred, spiritual, and ritual symbol in Buddhism, representing the universe and the celestial palace of a chosen deity. This particular mural depicts the mandala of **Avalokiteshvara**, the embodiment of the compassion of all Buddhas. It is not merely a painting but a two-dimensional architectural plan of a divine three-dimensional space, used as a focus for advanced meditation.

Creating a mandala is a sacred act of devotion, often undertaken by a group of monks over several days or weeks. Every geometric shape, line, and color is laden with deep symbolic meaning, laid out according to strict scriptural guidelines. Practitioners visualize themselves entering the mandala, traversing its gates, and arriving at the center, where they merge with the deity. This process is a powerful method for internalizing the qualities of compassion and wisdom.
